## Raw Concept

Chinese-language 36Kr article translating/summarizing expert views on **AI in game story and gameplay**: chatbot NPCs are often boring, expensive, lore-breaking, and ethically fraught; stronger use cases appear when LLMs are core mechanics or constrained components, not generic "in-game Siri."

## Narrative

### Core claims [TENTATIVE — expert commentary]

| Claim | castle-sim implication |
|-------|------------------------|
| Generic chatbot NPCs undermine authorial intent and often produce dull dialogue | Keep SH2-style lord messages scripted for v0–v2 |
| LLMs work better as **bounded components** for open input interpretation | Possible Tier 3+ "advisor" toy only after guardrails |
| Good runtime AI still needs writers, curated lore, privacy/cost controls | Do not treat runtime NPC AI as content-cost reduction |
| Human-authored games may become a premium signal | Stronghold-like hand-authored tone remains an asset |

Article cites narrative designers and AI researchers including Meg Jayanth, Max Kreminski, Dan Griliopoulos, Younès Rabii, and Failbetter's Chris Gardiner.

### Extract / skip

| Extract | Skip |
|---------|------|
| "AI-native" gameplay loop > bolting chatbot NPCs onto existing genres | Cloud chatbot peasants/lords in castle-sim |
| Authorial fences, curated world material, explicit reason for AI presence | Generic dialogue generation to reduce writing work |
| Treat runtime AI as a **system** needing design, tests, and costs | Replacing scripted Stronghold barks |

**Verdict:** **WATCH / DEFER** — update @concepts/llm-npc-runtime-ai-shelf.md and @concepts/agentic-npc-design-guardrails.md; no castle-sim brief because there is no near-term adoption.
